history of mathematics

The branch of study known as the history of mathematics is the investigation of the origins of discoveries in mathematics and, to a lesser extent, the investigation of mathematical methods and notation in the past. Before modern times and the spread of science throughout the world, written examples of the development of mathematics had shone in only a few places. The most ancient mathematical writings that have been discovered are Plimpton 322 (Babylonian mathematics around 1900 BC), the Rhind Mathematical Sheet (Egyptian mathematics around 2000-1800 BC) and the Moscow Mathematical Sheet (Egyptian mathematics around 1890 BC). All of these papers discuss a theorem commonly known as the Pythagorean theorem, which appears to be the oldest and most widespread mathematical development after elementary arithmetic and geometry. The contribution of Greek mathematicians refined methods (especially through the introduction of deductive reasoning and mathematical rigor in mathematical proof) and expanded the subject matter of mathematics. The word "mathematics" itself is derived from the ancient Greek word, μάθημα (mathema), which means "subject". Chinese mathematics made early contributions, including positional notation. The Hindu-Arabic number system and its operating rules, used today, were probably developed through lectures in the first millennium AD in Indian mathematics and were passed on to the West through Islamic mathematics. Islamic mathematics, in turn, developed and extended mathematical knowledge to this civilization. Many Greek and Arabic texts on mathematics were later translated into Latin, leading to the further development of mathematics in the European Middle Ages. From ancient times through the Middle Ages, bursts of mathematical creativity were often followed by centuries of stagnation. Beginning in the Italian Renaissance in the 16th century, the development of new mathematics, interacting with new scientific discoveries, led to exponential growth that continues to this day. Prehistoric mathematics Prehistoric mathematics refers to the development of mathematics in ancient times BC. The origins of mathematical thinking lie in the concepts of number, quantity and shapes. Modern studies of animal fossils show that this concept is not unique to humans. This concept may also be an everyday part of the hunting pack. That the concept of number evolved step by step over time is evident in that some contemporary languages preserve the distinction between "one", "two", and "many", but numbers greater than two do not. The oldest known mathematical object is the Lebombo bone, found in the Lebombo mountains in Swaziland and probably dating to 35,000 BC. This bone contains 29 different nicks that were intentionally scratched into the baboon's fibula. There is evidence that women used to count to remember their menstrual cycles; 28 to 30 scratches on bone or stone, followed by distinct markings. Also prehistoric artifacts found in Africa and France, dating to 35,000 BC and 20,000 years old, indicate early attempts to date. The Ishango bone, found near the waterhole of the Nile River (northeast Congo), contains a series of stick marks etched in three longitudinal stripes on the bone. The general interpretation is that the Ishango bones represent the oldest known demonstration of a sequence of prime numbers or a six-month lunar calendar. The Predynastic Period of Egypt from the 5th millennium BC, graphically displays geometric designs. It has been recognized that megalithic buildings in England and Scotland, from the 3rd millennium BC, incorporated geometric ideas such as circles, ellipses and Pythagorean triples in their designs.

Ancient Near East

Mesopotamaia

Mesopotamian mathematics is a mathematical system developed by ancient civilizations in the Mesopotamian region, which included the Sumerians, Akkadians, and Babylonians. This mathematics developed around 3000 to 1000 BC and has several main characteristics:

1. **Number System**: Mesopotamians used a base 60 (sexagesimal) number system, as opposed to the decimal (base 10) system more commonly used today. This system affects the division of time into 60 seconds per minute and 60 minutes per hour.

2. **Clay Texts and Tablets**: Many Mesopotamian mathematical records are found on clay tablets written in cuneiform. This tablet contains notes on calculations, mathematical problems, and geometric theory.

3. **Practical Calculations**: Mesopotamian mathematics was mainly used for practical purposes, such as managing agriculture, trade, and calculating taxes. They also developed methods for measuring length, area, and volume.

4. **Algebra and Geometry**: They already know the basic principles of algebra and geometry, such as solving quadratic equations and calculating the area and perimeter of various geometric shapes.

5. **Astronomy and Calendars**: Mesopotamian mathematicians also used mathematics to study astronomy and create calendars, including calculations of the movements of the moon and planets.

Mesopotamian mathematics had a major influence on the development of mathematics in subsequent civilizations, including Greek and ultimately European civilization.

Egypt

Egyptian mathematics is a mathematical system developed by the ancient Egyptian civilization around 3000 BC to 300 AD. It is known for its practical approach and applications in everyday life, including agriculture, architecture, and administration.

Following are some of the main characteristics of Egyptian mathematics:

1. **Number System**: The Egyptians used a decimal number system (base 10) with special symbols to represent ones, tens, hundreds, and so on. They also use special symbols for certain numbers such as 1, 10, 100, 1,000, and so on.

2. **Practical Calculations**: Egyptian mathematics was used for practical purposes, including tax calculations, food distribution, agricultural management, and the construction of large projects such as the pyramids.

3. **Arithmetic and Fractions**: They know basic arithmetic operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) and use fractions a lot, especially fractions with odd denominators.

4. **Geometry**: Egyptian mathematics developed the principles of geometry used in architecture and land measurement. They can calculate the area and perimeter of various shapes, such as rectangles and triangles.

5. **Mathematical Texts**: The Rhind Papyrus and the Moscow Papyrus are two famous Egyptian mathematical texts, containing examples of simple arithmetic, geometry and algebra problems.

Egyptian mathematics made a major contribution to the development of mathematics, especially in terms of measurement and practical approaches. These contributions later influenced Greek mathematics and other civilizations in the Mediterranean.

Greek Mathematics

Greek mathematics is a mathematical system developed by the ancient Greek civilization around the 6th to 4th centuries BC. Greek mathematics is considered one of the milestones in the history of mathematics because of its emphasis on logical proof and deductive approach. Following are some of the main characteristics of Greek mathematics:

1. **Geometry**: The Greeks are very famous for their development of geometry. Euclid, a famous mathematician, wrote *Elements*, which became one of the most influential mathematics books of all time and discussed geometry with a deductive approach.

2. **Arithmetics and Numbers**: Apart from geometry, Greek mathematics also developed arithmetic and number theory. Pythagoras is famous for the Pythagorean theorem, and he and his students developed the study of the relationship between number, form, and music.

3. **Proof Method**: Greek mathematics uses logical proof methods to solve problems and construct theorems. This approach became the basis for modern mathematics.

4. **Astronomy and Philosophy**: Greek mathematics is also closely related to astronomy and philosophy. For example, Hipparchus and Ptolemy combined mathematics with star observations to develop models of the solar system.

5. **Major Influence**: Greek mathematics provided an important basis for the development of mathematics in the Western world. Their works were translated and studied by Arab and European scientists during the Middle Ages, forming the basis of modern mathematics.

Greek mathematics focuses not only on practical applications, but also on the exploration of abstract concepts and mathematical theorems. This approach takes mathematics to a higher level as a systematic and logical science.

Chinese Mathematics

Chinese mathematics is a mathematical system developed in ancient China, starting around 3000 BC and lasting until the Qing dynasty (late 19th century). This mathematician is known for his achievements in calculations, geometry, and the development of numerical methods. Following are some of the main characteristics of Chinese mathematics:

1. **Number System**: Ancient China used a decimal number system (base 10) with special symbols to represent numbers from 0 to 9. They also developed methods to represent larger numbers.

2. **Mathematical Books**: Ancient Chinese mathematical works, such as "Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art" and "A New Mathematical System" (*The Mathematical Classic of Sunzi *), became the foundation for mathematical knowledge in China.

3. **Numerical Methods**: Chinese mathematics developed numerical methods to solve systems of linear equations and quadratic equations, such as the Gaussian elimination method.

4. **Arithmetic and Geometry**: Chinese mathematics includes basic arithmetic operations as well as an understanding of practical geometry for land measurement and construction.

5. **Practical Contribution**: Chinese mathematics has many practical applications in financial management, agriculture, and calendar making.

6. **Continuous Development**: Chinese mathematics has continued to develop over the centuries, with new discoveries enriching global science.

Chinese mathematics made a major contribution to the development of world mathematics, especially in numerical methods and calculation techniques. This knowledge also became the basis for the development of science in China and the surrounding region.

Indian Mathematics

Indian mathematics is a system of mathematics developed in the Indian subcontinent, beginning around 3000 BC and reaching its peak between the 5th and 12th centuries. Indian mathematics is known for innovations in arithmetic, algebra, and trigonometry, as well as the introduction of the decimal number system and the concept of zero. Following are some of the main characteristics of Indian mathematics:

1. **Decimal Number System**: Indian mathematics developed the decimal number system (base 10) which includes the number 0. This system became the basis of the number system used throughout the world today.

2. **Algebra**: Indian mathematics made major contributions to the development of algebra, including the introduction of the concept of negative numbers and the solution of quadratic equations.

3. **Trigonometry**: Ancient Indians developed tables of sines and cosines, as well as the relationships between angles and lines in triangles.

4. **Development of Number Theory**: Indian mathematics introduced important ideas in number theory, such as prime numbers and greatest common factors.

5. **Astronomy and Calendars**: Indian mathematics was used to develop calendar systems and astronomical calculations, including the determination of planetary positions and the movements of stars.

6. **Mathematical Books**: Indian mathematical works, such as *Aryabhatiya* by Aryabhata and *Lilavati* by Bhaskara, made a major contribution to the development of mathematics.

Indian mathematics has had a major influence on world mathematics, primarily through the spread of concepts and methods through trade and cultural exchange. Innovations such as the decimal number system and the concept of zero became the foundation of modern mathematics.
